# The Best Things to Do in Jeju: A Local's Guide to Korea's Island Paradise

If you've ever booked a flight to Jeju Island and then stared at your screen wondering, *"Okay… where do I even start?"* — you're not alone. Jeju is South Korea's largest island and its most beloved holiday destination, a volcanic wonderland off the southern coast where emerald beaches, dramatic crater peaks, and lush forest trails are packed into a space you can drive across in under two hours. The problem isn't finding things to do in Jeju; it's that there are *too many* options, and sorting the genuine highlights from the tourist traps takes real local know-how.

That's exactly what this guide is for. Whether you're planning a long weekend or a four-day escape, traveling solo, as a couple, with friends, or with kids in tow, I've pulled together the ten essential spots worth your time — organized by region so your route actually makes sense — along with photo tips, cost notes, and a ready-to-use itinerary. And the timing couldn't be better: early summer is when Jeju's famous hydrangeas hit full bloom and beach season officially kicks off.

I've cut the noise and kept only the places that earn a spot on the list. Read to the end and you'll have everything you need to build your own perfect Jeju trip.

## What to Know Before You Go

Jeju is roughly divided into four regions — east, west, south, and north — and understanding this layout is the single most useful thing you can do before arriving. The **east** is home to natural icons like Seongsan Ilchulbong and Udo Island. The **south**, centered on Seogwipo city, has waterfalls and volcanic cliffs. The **west** offers the island's most photogenic beaches and a cluster of seaside cafés in Aewol. The **north** is where you'll land — Jeju City and the airport are here.

Because these regions are spread out, **renting a car is essentially a must.** Public transport exists, but buses run infrequently and many of the best beaches and inland peaks are hard to reach without your own wheels. If you'd rather not drive, taxi tours and chauffeured package tours are solid alternatives.

The island also changes character with the seasons: canola flowers in spring, hydrangeas in early summer, silver pampas grass in autumn, and snow-dusted Hallasan in winter. Right now, in late June, the hydrangeas and beaches are at their absolute best.

One word of warning — peak summer means parking headaches and booking battles. Popular cafés, the Udo ferry, and certain experience venues fill up fast, so confirm reservations and check operating hours before you set out. A little planning here makes your whole route flow more smoothly.

## 1. Seongsan Ilchulbong (UNESCO World Heritage Site)

Towering over Jeju's eastern coast, Seongsan Ilchulbong — often called "Sunrise Peak" — is a UNESCO World Natural Heritage site formed by a volcanic eruption thousands of years ago. Rising straight out of the sea like a giant crown, its crater ridge creates one of the most jaw-dropping views on the island. It's famous for sunrises, true to its name, but it's every bit as spectacular in daylight.

The hike to the summit is a steep stairway that takes about 25 minutes on foot. It's not strenuous, but there's little shade, so bring a hat and water and aim for early morning or late afternoon in summer. There's a small admission fee for adults — check the official site for current pricing and hours before you visit.

On your way down, don't miss the **haenyeo** performance at Udumgae Beach, where Jeju's legendary female free-divers demonstrate their centuries-old diving tradition without modern breathing gear. It's a rare, living glimpse of the island's culture — and it cements Seongsan Ilchulbong as the number-one thing to do in Jeju.

## 2. Udo Island (The "Cow Island")

Named for its resemblance to a reclining cow, Udo is a tiny island just a 15-minute ferry ride from Seongsan Port. Ferries generally run at regular intervals from morning to evening, but schedules shift with the season and weather, so always check same-day departure times. During peak season, bringing your own car may be restricted — most visitors rent an electric buggy or bike on the island instead.

Udo's calling card is its water. The black volcanic sand of Geommeolle Beach contrasts dramatically with the turquoise sea, while Hagosudong Beach pairs white sand with crystal-clear shallows — a guaranteed photo spot. Follow the coastal road that circles the island and you'll find a postcard view around nearly every bend.

Don't leave without trying the local peanut ice cream, made from Udo's famous peanuts. Easily a half-day trip, Udo is one of the most rewarding island-hopping experiences on any list of things to do in Jeju.

## 3–5. Natural Wonders: Hallasan, Cheonjiyeon Falls & the Columnar Joints

**Hallasan** is the beating heart of Jeju — South Korea's tallest mountain and a dormant volcano. For beginners, the Yeongsil or Eorimok trails are ideal. Yeongsil is shorter and rewards you with striking rock formations and sweeping views, while Eorimok's gentle ridges suit families. The full route to the Baengnokdam crater lake at the summit may require an advance reservation, so confirm depending on your plans.

**Cheonjiyeon Waterfall** sits close to downtown Seogwipo, making it wonderfully accessible. A walk through dense subtropical forest leads to a powerful cascade, and during evening hours the falls are lit up for a dreamier atmosphere.

**Daepo Jusangjeolli** — the Columnar Joint cliffs — formed as volcanic lava cooled into perfect hexagonal stone pillars stacked along the coast. With waves crashing against the basalt and well-built viewing platforms, it's a stunning natural sculpture and a fantastic photo backdrop.

## 6–8. For the Aesthetes: Volcanic Peaks, Cafés & Sea Views

Jeju's most atmospheric side reveals itself on its **oreum** — the small parasitic volcanic cones scattered across the island. **Saebyeol Oreum** (#6) is a local favorite, with gentle grassy slopes, waves of pampas grass, and panoramic views in every direction from the top. Nearby **Abu Oreum** features a ring of cedar trees encircling its crater, making the view from above genuinely unique. Best of all, both are completely free to enter.

**Hyeopjae and Geumneung Beaches** (#7) define Jeju's west coast: turquoise water and white sand framed by the silhouette of Biyangdo Island offshore. In summer they're prime swimming spots and photo destinations alike.

**Aewol's seaside café strip** (#8) is a hit with couples and friend groups. A row of cafés with floor-to-ceiling windows looks straight out over the ocean — visit around sunset and the mood is unbeatable. If you're hunting for the most Instagram-worthy things to do in Jeju, build this region into your route.

## 9–10. Family-Friendly Fun: Forests, Theme Parks & Rainy-Day Backups

Traveling with kids? **Bijarim Forest** (#9) is a flat, stroller-friendly trail winding through nutmeg trees that are hundreds of years old. Just breathing the phytoncide-rich air as you stroll is restorative. Pair it with **ECOLAND**, a theme park where a vintage train carries you through the primeval Gotjawal forest — a perennial kid-pleaser.

Spot #10 is your rainy-day insurance. **Aquaplanet Jeju** is one of Korea's largest aquariums, putting an incredible range of marine life within arm's reach. Add museums and indoor theme parks scattered across every region, and you've got plenty of backup plans for Jeju's notoriously fickle weather.

A smart move for families: pair each outdoor stop with an indoor alternative in advance, so a sudden shower never derails your day.

## Where to Eat: Jeju's Must-Try Local Flavors

It's no exaggeration to say half the joy of a Jeju trip is the food. The signature dish is **heukdwaeji** — grilled Jeju black pork, prized for its rich flavor and chew. Dedicated black-pork streets in both Jeju City and Seogwipo make it easy to find a great spot near your hotel. For something heartier, try **gogi-guksu**, thick noodles in a deep pork-bone broth topped with generous slices of meat — a true island specialty.

Heading to the southern Seogwipo region? Order **galchi** (beltfish), either braised or grilled. A whole silver beltfish braised in spicy seasoning is a delicacy you'll find few places outside Jeju. And a stop at a traditional market like Dongmun Market gets you fresh sashimi, sweet shrimp, and squid at reasonable prices.

The key to a good itinerary is pairing attractions with food. See Seongsan Ilchulbong in the east, then lunch at a nearby haenyeo-run seafood restaurant; enjoy Hyeopjae Beach in the west, then dessert at an Aewol café. Clustering like this saves serious driving time.

## A 3-Day Itinerary (No Wasted Time)

The most efficient way to tackle the best things to do in Jeju is to group your stops by region. Starting from the airport, flow **east → south → west** so your route never doubles back.

**Day 1 (East):** Pick up your rental car and head east. Stroll Bijarim Forest → hike Seongsan Ilchulbong → take a half-day Udo tour. Stay overnight near Seongsan and have dinner at a haenyeo seafood restaurant.

**Day 2 (South):** Visit Cheonjiyeon Waterfall and the Daepo Columnar Joints, then enjoy braised beltfish for lunch in Seogwipo. In the afternoon, hike Hallasan's Yeongsil trail — or simply relax at a café, depending on your energy.

**Day 3 (West):** Morning walk at Saebyeol Oreum → swim at Hyeopjae and Geumneung Beaches → catch the sunset from an Aewol seaside café before returning to the airport.

That's three to four stops a day, realistically paced with driving time factored in. With a fourth day, add a half-day of slack to each region to dig deeper into the cafés and restaurants.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How many days do I need to see the top things to do in Jeju?**
A: Since the highlights are spread across all four regions, three days lets you hit one or two key spots per region — the east → south → west route above is the classic approach. Four days gives you breathing room for cafés, restaurants, and an extra oreum or two. Resist the urge to cram in five-plus stops a day; you'll burn out on driving and won't even get good photos. Three to four stops daily is the sweet spot.

**Q: Can I get around Jeju without a rental car?**
A: It's possible, but far less efficient. City buses and tourist loop buses exist, yet intervals are long and outlying peaks and beaches are tough to reach. If you can drive, a rental car is by far the easiest option. If you don't have a license, taxi tours or chauffeured package tours are good alternatives — they cost more but spare you the logistics and the fatigue.

**Q: When is the best season to visit Jeju?**
A: Each season has its own appeal: canola flowers in spring, hydrangeas and beaches in early summer (right now), pampas grass on the oreum in autumn, and snowy Hallasan in winter. Just note that summer peak season and holidays bring the biggest crowds and the highest lodging prices — if you want quiet, target weekdays or the off-season.

**Q: Are any of the top things to do in Jeju free?**
A: Plenty. Hyeopjae and Geumneung Beaches, oreum like Saebyeol and Abu, and several columnar-joint viewpoints are free to enjoy. Sites like Seongsan Ilchulbong, Cheonjiyeon Waterfall, and Aquaplanet do charge admission, generally in the small-to-moderate range for adults. Mixing in the free spots lets you build a great, budget-friendly itinerary.

## Final Thoughts

We've now covered the ten best things to do in Jeju — from Seongsan Ilchulbong and Udo Island to Hallasan, Hyeopjae Beach, and Saebyeol Oreum, all organized by region. The takeaway is simple: cluster your stops by area to plan a tight route, pair every outdoor attraction with an indoor backup, and connect each sight with nearby restaurants and cafés. Stick to those three principles and you'll have a full, efficient trip with no wasted time.

With the hydrangeas in full bloom and beach season just opening up, there's no better moment to experience Jeju. Pick the spots that speak to you, build your own three-day route, and head off for an island adventure full of unforgettable views. Happy travels!
