# Best Places to Visit in Jeju: 10 Spots Locals Love (With Routes, Fees & Photo Tips)

Jeju Island is South Korea's answer to Hawaii — a volcanic paradise an hour's flight south of Seoul, ringed by emerald beaches, crowned by a dormant volcano, and dotted with waterfalls, lava cliffs, and grassy craters called *oreum*. If you've already booked your tickets and found yourself staring at a map thinking, "Where do I even start?", you're not alone. The whole island feels like one giant attraction, and that's exactly the problem: too many options, not enough clarity on which ones are actually worth your time.

This guide cuts through the noise. Whether you're traveling as a couple, a family, or a group of friends, I've rounded up the ten best places to visit in Jeju, organized by region so you can build an efficient route instead of zigzagging across the island. You'll find photo-worthy viewpoints, honest budget notes, local food pairings, and a ready-to-use 3-day itinerary. Early summer is one of the best windows of the year to go — the hydrangeas are in full bloom and beach season has just opened — so your timing couldn't be better.

I've left out the filler and kept only the spots that genuinely earn a place on your list. Read to the end and you'll have everything you need to build your own perfect Jeju trip.

## Before You Go: Jeju Travel Basics

The best places to visit in Jeju are spread across four regions — east, west, south, and north. The **east** is home to natural icons like Seongsan Ilchulbong, Udo Island, and the Bijarim forest. The **south** centers on Seogwipo city, with Cheonjiyeon Falls and the dramatic Jusangjeolli lava columns. The **west** is beach-and-café country, with Hyeopjae and Geumneung beaches and the cliffside cafés of Aewol. The **north** is where you'll land — Jeju City and the airport.

Because these regions are spread so far apart, **renting a car is practically essential.** Public transport exists, but buses run infrequently and many of the best viewpoints sit well outside city centers. With a car, you can comfortably hit three or four spots a day; without one, that number drops sharply. If you don't drive, a private taxi tour or a chauffeured package tour is the realistic alternative.

Each season has its own signature look. Spring brings golden canola fields, early summer (right now) peaks with hydrangeas and beach weather, autumn turns the hillsides silver with pampas grass, and winter dusts Hallasan Mountain with snow. At the moment, the hydrangea spots and beaches are at their absolute best.

One practical heads-up: peak summer means crowded parking lots and booked-out reservations. Popular cafés, the Udo ferry, and some experience venues can fill up fast, so confirm availability before you go. A quick check of each spot's parking situation and opening hours before you leave will keep your route running smoothly.

## #1 — Seongsan Ilchulbong (UNESCO World Natural Heritage)

The undisputed icon of eastern Jeju, Seongsan Ilchulbong is a volcanic tuff cone that rises straight out of the sea like a giant crown. It's listed as a UNESCO World Natural Heritage Site, and the moment you see that jagged green ridgeline against the ocean, you'll understand why. The name means "Sunrise Peak," and yes, it's a legendary sunrise spot — but it's just as spectacular in broad daylight.

The hike to the summit takes about 25 minutes up a steep staircase. It's not a brutal climb, but there's little shade along the way, so in summer bring a hat and water and aim for early morning or late afternoon. Admission is modest (a small per-adult fee), though it's always worth checking the official site for current rates and hours before your visit.

Back at the bottom, don't miss the *haenyeo* diving demonstration at Useongmae Coast. These free-diving women — many in their 60s, 70s, and beyond — harvest seafood without oxygen tanks, a tradition so culturally significant that UNESCO recognized it too. Watching them work up close is a glimpse of living Jeju heritage, and it cements Seongsan Ilchulbong as the number-one stop on any list of the best places to visit in Jeju.

## #2 — Udo Island (The "Cow Island")

Named for its resemblance to a reclining cow, Udo is a tiny island just a 15-minute ferry ride from Seongsan Port. Ferries generally run at regular intervals from morning to evening, but schedules shift with the season and weather, so always confirm the day's sailings before you commit. During peak season, bringing your own car may be restricted, which is why most visitors rent an electric buggy or bicycle once they arrive.

Udo's real magic is the color of its water. At Geommeolle Beach, jet-black volcanic sand meets turquoise surf in a striking contrast, while Hagosudong Beach pairs powdery white sand with crystal-clear shallows — a top photo spot. Follow the coastal road that circles the island and you'll find a postcard-perfect frame around almost every bend.

Be sure to try the peanut ice cream, made from Udo's famous local peanuts — a small, sweet ritual that's become part of the experience. A half day here is the perfect amount of time, and it earns Udo a firm spot among the best places to visit in Jeju.

## #3–5 — Natural Wonders (Hallasan, Cheonjiyeon Falls & Jusangjeolli)

**#3 Hallasan Mountain** is the beating heart of the island — South Korea's highest peak at 1,947 meters. For first-timers, the Yeongsil or Eorimok trails are the way to go. Yeongsil is shorter and rewards you with dramatic rock formations and sweeping views; Eorimok climbs a gentler ridge that's manageable even for families. The trails leading all the way to the Baengnokdam crater lake may require an advance reservation, so check ahead depending on your plans.

**#4 Cheonjiyeon Falls** scores points for accessibility, sitting just a short walk from downtown Seogwipo. A shaded path through lush subtropical forest leads you to a wide curtain of water crashing into a deep pool. When the falls are lit up during evening hours, the whole scene takes on a dreamy, almost otherworldly glow.

**#5 Jusangjeolli Cliffs** is where cooling lava froze into thousands of hexagonal stone pillars stacked along the coast — nature's own version of cut masonry. With waves smashing against the columns and well-built viewing platforms, it's an effortless place to capture a dramatic shot of geology in action.

## #6–8 — Scenic & Stylish Spots (Oreum, Cafés & Sea Views)

Jeju's most atmospheric side reveals itself on the *oreum* — the small volcanic cones scattered across the island. **#6 Saebyeol Oreum** is beloved for its gentle, grassy slopes and the 360-degree panorama from the top. Abu Oreum, nearby, is famous for the perfect ring of cedar trees encircling its crater — a uniquely satisfying view from above. Best of all, both are completely free to enter, making them unbeatable value.

**#7 Hyeopjae and Geumneung Beaches** deliver the classic western-Jeju postcard: turquoise water and white sand with the tiny islet of Biyangdo floating on the horizon. In summer they double as swimming spots and photo backdrops, and they're consistently among the most popular places on the island.

**#8 The Aewol seaside café strip** is a favorite with couples and friends. A run of cafés with floor-to-ceiling windows opens right onto the ocean, and if you time your visit for sunset, the atmosphere is hard to beat. If you're hunting for the more stylish, slow-paced side of the best places to visit in Jeju, build this stretch of coast into your plans.

## #9–10 — Family-Friendly Picks (Theme Parks & Hands-On Fun)

Traveling with kids? **#9 Bijarim Forest and Eco Land** are your best bets. Bijarim is a centuries-old nutmeg-tree forest with flat, stroller-friendly paths — just breathing in the phytoncide-rich air as you wander is its own kind of therapy. Eco Land is a theme park where a vintage steam train carries you through the *Gotjawal* primeval forest, a ride children especially love.

**#10 is your rainy-day insurance.** Aquaplanet Jeju is one of Korea's largest aquariums, packed with marine life you can get surprisingly close to. Beyond it, museums and indoor theme parks are scattered across every region — invaluable backups when Jeju's famously fickle weather turns on you.

When mapping out family-friendly places to visit in Jeju, the smart move is to pair each outdoor spot with an indoor alternative nearby, so a sudden downpour never derails your day.

## Local Food & Where to Eat Along the Way

It's no exaggeration to say half of any Jeju trip is the food. The signature dish is **black pork barbecue** (*heuk-dwaeji*) — succulent, richly marbled pork grilled at your table. Whole streets in Jeju City and Seogwipo are dedicated to it, so you'll find a spot near almost any accommodation. For something heartier, *gogi-guksu* — thick wheat noodles in a deep, slow-simmered pork-bone broth, topped with generous slices of meat — is a Jeju specialty you won't find done quite the same anywhere else.

If you're exploring the southern Seogwipo region, order the **braised or grilled hairtail** (*galchi*). A whole silver hairtail, braised in a spicy-savory sauce, is a coastal delicacy you can really only enjoy properly here. Stop by a traditional market like Dongmun Market and you'll find sashimi, sweet shrimp, and cuttlefish at reasonable prices.

The key to planning is bundling attractions with restaurants. See Seongsan Ilchulbong in the east, then grab lunch at a nearby *haenyeo* restaurant. Enjoy Hyeopjae Beach in the west, then linger over dessert at an Aewol café. Linking your stops this way cuts your driving time dramatically.

## A Zero-Waste 3-Day Itinerary

An efficient route through the best places to visit in Jeju starts with grouping by region. From the airport, the natural flow is **east → south → west**, which keeps you from doubling back.

**Day 1 (East):** Land, pick up your rental car, and head east. Walk the Bijarim forest trail → hike Seongsan Ilchulbong → take a half-day Udo Island tour. Stay overnight near Seongsan and have dinner at a *haenyeo* restaurant.

**Day 2 (South):** See Cheonjiyeon Falls and the Jusangjeolli Cliffs, then enjoy braised hairtail for lunch in Seogwipo. In the afternoon, tackle the Yeongsil trail on Hallasan — or, depending on your energy, swap it for a relaxed café break.

**Day 3 (West):** Start with a morning walk up Saebyeol Oreum → swim at Hyeopjae or Geumneung Beach → catch the sunset at an Aewol seaside café before heading back to the airport.

That's three or four spots a day — a realistic pace once you factor in driving. If you have a fourth day, add a half-day buffer to each region so you can dig deeper into the cafés and food.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How many days do I need to see the best places to visit in Jeju?**
A: Because the top ten are spread across all four regions, three days (two nights) is enough to hit one or two highlights per region — the east → south → west route above is the proven template. With four days, you can layer in extra cafés, restaurants, and a bonus oreum at a relaxed pace. Resist the urge to cram in five-plus spots a day; you'll burn out on driving and won't even get good photos. Three to four per day is the sweet spot.

**Q: Can I get around Jeju without a rental car?**
A: You can, but it's far less efficient. Local and tourist loop buses exist, yet they run infrequently and rarely reach the outlying oreum and beaches. If you can drive, a rental car is by far the easiest option. If you don't have a license, a taxi tour or chauffeured package tour is the alternative — it costs more, but it spares you the planning and the fatigue.

**Q: What's the best season to visit Jeju?**
A: Every season has its draw: canola flowers in spring, hydrangeas and beaches in early summer (right now), pampas grass on Saebyeol Oreum in autumn, and snow-capped Hallasan in winter. Just note that peak summer and holidays bring the biggest crowds and the highest accommodation prices — if you want quieter trails, target weekdays or the shoulder seasons.

**Q: Are any of the best places to visit in Jeju free?**
A: Plenty. Hyeopjae and Geumneung Beaches, the Saebyeol and Abu oreum, and some lava-column viewpoints cost nothing. Paid spots like Seongsan Ilchulbong, Cheonjiyeon Falls, and Aquaplanet generally charge a modest-to-moderate per-adult fee. Mixing in a few free attractions is the easiest way to keep your budget in check without missing out.

## Final Thoughts

We've covered the ten best places to visit in Jeju, from Seongsan Ilchulbong and Udo Island to Hallasan, Hyeopjae Beach, and Saebyeol Oreum, all organized by region. The strategy boils down to three simple rules: group your stops by region to build a tight route, pair every outdoor attraction with an indoor backup, and link nearby restaurants and cafés into your plan. Stick to those three and you'll have a packed, frustration-free trip.

With the hydrangeas blooming and the beaches just opening, there's no better time to go. Pick the spots that excite you most, build your own two- or three-night route, and head off for a Jeju adventure full of unforgettable views. Safe travels — and enjoy every minute of it!
