# The 10 Best Things to Do in Jeju: A Must-See Itinerary You'll Regret Skipping

Summer travel season is here, and if you're still mapping out where to go, Jeju Island deserves a spot at the top of your list. As of late June 2026, Jeju has officially slipped into its rainy monsoon stretch. Showers come and go, but there's a silver lining: the mountain valleys and waterfalls of Hallasan are running full and refreshingly cool right now. Once the rains clear in mid-July, the island rolls straight into peak beach season — which makes this exact moment the smart time to lock in your flights and accommodation before prices climb.

Whether it's your first trip to Korea's biggest island or your fifth time circling the same familiar loop, the question is always the same: with limited days, where do you go first so you don't leave with regrets? This guide is built to answer exactly that. We've organized the best **things to do in Jeju** by region — perfect for families, couples, and friend groups alike — and bundled it all into a tight three-day, two-night itinerary that actually flows.

Search "Jeju" online and you'll drown in hundreds of options. The real skill is filtering down to the spots that genuinely earn your time. Stick with the top 10 and the route below, and you'll travel smarter — no wasted hours, no wasted energy, no wasted budget. Read to the end and build a course that's all your own.

## Why These 10? How We Picked the Best Things to Do in Jeju

Out of countless attractions, three criteria shaped this shortlist. First, **accessibility** — each place is easy to reach by rental car or public transit, and easy to pair with nearby stops so your route makes sense. Second, **photo payoff** — these are the spots that consistently light up social feeds, where a single shot captures the whole mood of your trip. Third, **the return factor** — we favored places that travelers describe as "I'd happily come back again," not one-and-done tourist traps.

To keep you from burning daylight on the road, we spread the picks across four zones: **East, West, South, and Jeju City**. Jeju is deceptively large, and crisscrossing the island can swallow half a day in transit alone. So we've grouped nearby attractions together to keep your driving sane.

Finally, we mixed **paid and free** sites along with **indoor and outdoor** options. With monsoon rain and sudden summer downpours always a possibility, it pays to have a rain-friendly backup ready when an outdoor plan falls through. On a summer trip to a weather-volatile island, that flexibility matters more than anything.

## Ranks 1–3: If It's Your First Time in Jeju, These Are Non-Negotiable

**#1 Seongsan Ilchulbong (Sunrise Peak)** is a UNESCO World Natural Heritage site and the icon of eastern Jeju. This dramatic volcanic crater rises straight out of the sea, and a 20–30 minute climb to the summit rewards you with a wide-open panorama. As the name suggests, it's a legendary sunrise spot — but it's just as striking in broad daylight. If you're chasing the sunrise, remember the sun comes up early in summer, so arrive before dawn. The nearby Gwangchigi Beach makes an easy add-on at the base.

**#2 Seopjikoji** sits about 10 minutes by car from Sunrise Peak, making the two an obvious pairing. A coastal walking trail, a picturesque lighthouse, and architecture made famous by Korean dramas and films come together for some of the best photos on the island. The wind here can be fierce, so a hat or a light jacket will keep you comfortable.

**#3 Udo Island** is a tiny island reachable by a roughly 15-minute ferry from Seongsan Port. Most visitors rent an electric car or scooter to loop the island, and the local specialty — peanut ice cream and peanut makgeolli (a rice wine), both made from Udo's famous peanuts — is not to be missed. Ferries get crowded in peak summer, so check the round-trip schedule in advance. Clustered together in the east, these three are tailor-made for a single day's circuit, and they top any list of **things to do in Jeju**.

## Ranks 4–6: The Ultimate Natural Scenery Course

**#4 Hallasan (Mount Halla)** is the crown jewel of any Jeju trip. The Seongpanak and Gwaneumsa trails leading all the way to the Baengnokdam crater at the summit are physically demanding, so beginners should opt for the **Eorimok** or **Yeongsil** routes instead. Both climb relatively gently to the Witse Oreum plateau, making them doable even for families. Eorimok runs a little longer, while Yeongsil is known for its stunning craggy cliff scenery. Mountain weather can shift fast in summer, so always check the trail entry cutoff times and the forecast before you head up.

**#5 Cheonjiyeon and Jeongbang Waterfalls** handle the "stay cool" department down south in Seogwipo. Cheonjiyeon has a flat, easy walking path from the entrance, while Jeongbang is one of the rare waterfalls in Asia that pours directly into the sea. During monsoon season the water volume swells, making both falls feel even more majestic — which means summer is actually a prime time to see them.

**#6 Jusangjeolli Cliffs at Daepo, Jungmun** is where you'll watch waves crash against towering hexagonal basalt columns. The shots get more dramatic when the surf is high, so aim for a windy day — just be sure to shoot from inside the safety railings. If raw, untamed coastal scenery is what you're after, this stop delivers.

## Ranks 7–10: Hidden Gems & Atmospheric Spots

**#7 Camellia Hill and Bijarim Forest** are the gold standard for a healing stroll. Camellia Hill is an arboretum bursting with camellias and countless other plants, with a different mood in every season, while Bijarim lets you wander a forest of centuries-old nutmeg trees that practically clears your head on contact. Both offer plenty of shade, staying relatively cool even in the heat of summer — a great outdoor option for beating the warmth.

**#8 Aewol and Handam Coastal Walk** is a runaway favorite among couples and friend groups. The Handam coastal path hugs an emerald-blue sea, and the stretch is lined with stylish cafés that practically guarantee a frame-worthy photo. Catch it at sunset and the golden light makes everything glow. As the signature atmospheric pick of the west, it never misses on a roundup of the best **things to do in Jeju**.

**#9 Osulloc Tea Museum and Green Tea Fields** lets you soak in sweeping plantation views while sampling green tea desserts. Pair it with the Innisfree Jeju House right next door for an easy half-day course. **For #10**, we're leaving the slot open for a local favorite in whichever region you land in. Working a market full of regional eats into your plans — like the Dongmun Market night market or Seogwipo's Maeil Olle Market — instantly boosts the satisfaction of any trip.

## The Ideal 3-Day, 2-Night Route (Optimized by Region)

Hop between distant zones and you'll spend the whole trip on the road. That's why we recommend a clockwise loop running **East → South → West**. **Day 1:** Head from the airport to the east, bundle Sunrise Peak, Seopjikoji, and Udo together, and stay overnight near Seongsan. **Day 2:** Drop down south for Hallasan (via Eorimok or Yeongsil) or the waterfall course, then spend the night in Seogwipo. **Day 3:** Explore Aewol and Osulloc in the west before circling back to the airport.

For getting around, a **rental car** is by far the most convenient choice. Many of the best attractions sit on the outskirts where bus service runs infrequently, so if you're traveling with at least one other person, a rental usually wins on both time and cost. If you're solo or not keen on driving, you can still build a solid trip around Jeju City and the east using buses and taxis.

Always keep a **Plan B** ready for rainy days. Lining up indoor options in advance — the Osulloc Tea Museum, the Jeju Folk Village, and the island's many indoor museums and aquariums — means a sudden monsoon downpour won't derail your day. Alternating outdoor and indoor stops is the secret to a smooth summer itinerary in Jeju.

## Travel Tips & Budget Breakdown

Peak summer is when flights, rental cars, and accommodation all sell out fast and prices spike. **Late July through early August** is especially competitive, since it's vacation season across all of Korea. Book flights at least a month out, and grab popular accommodations and rental cars even earlier to be safe. Slotting in a weekday departure can ease the price pressure compared to weekends.

Costs vary by region, but **admission fees** at the major attractions range from free to around 10,000 won (roughly $7–8). Places like Sunrise Peak, Bijarim, and Osulloc charge entry, while coastal walks and café streets are free. **Parking** is mostly free or just a small fee, so it won't weigh on your budget much. Set aside a generous amount for meals and cafés, then calculate activity costs — like the Udo ferry or a Hallasan hike — separately to make budgeting painless.

Don't forget your summer Jeju essentials. The sun is strong, so **sunscreen, a hat, and sunglasses** are basics, and during monsoon season an **umbrella or rain poncho** is a must. When you need a ride, having a ride-hailing app like Kakao T installed ahead of time makes hailing a taxi far easier. These small preparations are what round out a rewarding trip and unlock the best **things to do in Jeju**.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Can I hit all 10 in a 3-day, 2-night trip?**
Honestly, cramming in everything is a stretch. For three days and two nights, we recommend narrowing your focus to about **6 or 7 spots** within tighter zones. Pick the highlights — Sunrise Peak, Seopjikoji, and Udo in the east; a waterfall or Hallasan in the south; Aewol and Osulloc in the west — and travel at a relaxed pace. Selective and unhurried beats a frantic full sweep every time.

**Q: Is a Jeju trip doable without a rental car?**
It's possible, but it depends on the region. Jeju City and parts of the east are perfectly navigable by bus and taxi, but **the outer west and quieter attractions** have sparse service, so a rental car is recommended there. If you're traveling with at least one other person and plan to hit a lot of outlying spots, a rental tends to win on both time and money.

**Q: What's the best season for summer Jeju, and what should I avoid?**
Late June through mid-July is **monsoon** season, so rain is a real wildcard. The upside is thinner crowds and relatively lower prices. Early August is full-on peak season — busy and pricier. So if you want peace and quiet, target the tail end of the rainy season; if you want energy and buzz, go for peak summer.

**Q: Which Jeju spots are great with kids?**
**Udo** is easy with children since you can cruise it slowly by electric car, and indoor theme parks, aquariums, and museums are weather-proof crowd-pleasers. Strenuous climbs like Hallasan are tough with little ones, so lean toward flat, gentle walking paths when planning your route.
