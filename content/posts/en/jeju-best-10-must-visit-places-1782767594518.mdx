# Best Things to Do in Jeju: 10 Must-See Spots You'll Regret Missing

Planning your summer getaway? Now's the time to lock it in. As of late June 2026, Jeju Island has officially slipped into its rainy season — the *jangma*, Korea's early-summer monsoon. Yes, you'll get those on-and-off showers, but here's the upside: Hallasan's valleys and waterfalls are gushing at full force right now, and the moment the rains clear in mid-July, peak beach season kicks off instantly. In other words, this is the perfect window to grab your flights and book your stay before everyone else does.

Whether it's your first trip to Korea's largest island or your fifth, the question is always the same: *"With limited time, where do I start so I don't miss anything?"* This guide is the answer. I've pulled together the best **things to do in Jeju** — organized by region so you waste zero time on the road — along with a ready-made 3-day route that works for families, couples, and friend groups alike.

Search "Jeju" and you'll drown in hundreds of options. The real skill is filtering down to the spots actually worth your time. Stick with the ten below plus the suggested loop, and you'll travel smart — no wasted hours, no burned-out legs, no blown budget. Read to the end and build your own perfect itinerary.

## Why These 10? (How I Picked Them)

Out of countless attractions, these ten made the cut on three criteria. **First, accessibility.** Each is easy to reach by rental car or public transit, and pairs naturally with nearby sights so you can chain them into a clean route. **Second, photo payoff.** These are the spots that consistently light up Instagram — the ones where a single frame sells the whole vibe of your trip. **Third, the "I'd come back" factor.** I leaned toward places that travelers rave about returning to, not one-and-done tourist traps.

To cut down on driving time, I spread the list evenly across four zones — **East, West, South, and Jeju City**. People underestimate how long it takes to cross the island; bounce between far-flung regions and you'll burn half a day staring at the road. So I've grouped nearby spots together.

Finally, I mixed **paid and free** sites along with **indoor and outdoor** ones. When monsoon rain or a sudden summer downpour wrecks your outdoor plans, you'll want a backup. That's why I've folded in rainy-day indoor alternatives throughout — a detail that matters more than usual on weather-volatile summer Jeju.

## #1–3: First Time in Jeju? Don't Skip These

**#1 — Seongsan Ilchulbong (Sunrise Peak)** is a UNESCO World Natural Heritage site and the icon of eastern Jeju. This dramatic volcanic crater rises straight out of the sea, and the roughly 20–30 minute climb to the top rewards you with a wide-open panorama. As the name "Sunrise Peak" suggests, it's a legendary spot to catch the dawn — but it's just as stunning by day. If you're chasing the sunrise, note that summer days break early here, so arrive before first light. The nearby Gwangchigi Beach is an easy add-on at the base.

**#2 — Seopjikoji** sits just a 10-minute drive from Sunrise Peak, making the two a natural pairing. A seaside walking trail, a photogenic lighthouse, and the famous architecture used as backdrops in Korean dramas and films all come together for picture-perfect moments. It gets windy out on the cape, so a hat or light jacket will keep you comfortable.

**#3 — Udo (Cow Island)** is a tiny island just a 15-minute ferry ride from Seongsan Port. The classic move is to rent an electric car or scooter and loop the whole island, stopping for the local specialty: peanut ice cream and *makgeolli* (rice wine) made from Udo-grown peanuts. Ferries get packed during peak summer, so check the round-trip schedule in advance. Clustered together in the east, these three are tailor-made for a single full-day route — and they're among the most rewarding **things to do in Jeju** for first-timers.

## #4–6: Nature at Its Most Spectacular

**#4 — Hallasan** is the crown jewel of any Jeju trip. The Seongpanak and Gwaneumsa trails to the summit crater, Baengnokdam, demand serious stamina — so if you're a beginner, take the **Eorimok** or **Yeongsil** routes instead. Both climb at a gentler grade to Witseoreum, making them doable even for families. Eorimok runs a bit longer, while Yeongsil is prized for its dramatic cliff scenery. Mountain weather can flip in an instant during summer, so always confirm entry-time cutoffs and the forecast before you head up.

**#5 — Cheonjiyeon and Jeongbang Waterfalls** are the south's answer to summer heat, near Seogwipo. Cheonjiyeon is an easy, flat stroll from the entrance, while Jeongbang is one of the rare waterfalls that plunges directly into the sea. During monsoon season the water volume swells, making them feel even more majestic — so summer is actually prime waterfall-viewing time.

**#6 — Jusangjeolli Cliffs at Daepo (Jungmun)** is where you'll watch waves crash against towering hexagonal basalt columns. The shots get more dramatic when the surf is high, so aim for a windy day — just be sure to shoot from inside the safety railings. If you're after raw, untouched scenery, this one delivers.

## #7–10: Hidden Gems & Aesthetic Spots

**#7 — Camellia Hill and Bijarim Forest** are the gold standard for a restorative walk. Camellia Hill is an arboretum brimming with camellias and countless other plants, with a different mood in every season. Bijarim is a forest of centuries-old nutmeg-yew trees, where simply walking the path clears your head. Both offer plenty of shade, staying relatively cool even in midsummer — ideal outdoor escapes from the heat.

**#8 — The Aewol & Handam Coastal Trail** is a runaway favorite with couples and friend groups. The Handam coastal path hugs an emerald-blue shoreline, lined with a stylish café street that's overflowing with photo ops. Time it for sunset and the golden-hour glow makes it impossibly pretty. As western Jeju's signature mood spot, it's a fixture on any list of **things to do in Jeju**.

**#9 — Osulloc Tea Museum and the Green Tea Fields** lets you soak in sweeping plantation views while enjoying green-tea desserts. Pair it with the Innisfree Jeju House right next door for an easy half-day. **And for #10**, I'm leaving room for a local favorite in whichever region you land in — think a traditional market loaded with regional eats, like the night market at Dongmun Market or Seogwipo's Maeil Olle Market. Slot one of these into your itinerary and watch your satisfaction with the whole trip jump.

## The Ideal 3-Day Route (Region by Region)

Crisscross the island and you'll spend your whole trip in the car. So I recommend a clockwise loop that flows **East → South → West**. **Day 1:** Head from the airport to the east and chain Seongsan Ilchulbong, Seopjikoji, and Udo, then stay overnight near Seongsan. **Day 2:** Drop south for Hallasan (Eorimok or Yeongsil) or the waterfall route, and spend the night in Seogwipo. **Day 3:** Explore western Aewol and Osulloc, then circle back to the airport.

For getting around, a **rental car** is by far the easiest option. Many of the best sights sit on the outskirts where bus service runs sparse, so if there are two or more of you, a rental usually wins on both time and cost. On the other hand, if you're solo or not keen on driving, you can stick to Jeju City and the eastern zone and lean on buses and taxis.

Don't forget a **Plan B for rainy days**. Scout out indoor options ahead of time — the Osulloc Tea Museum, the Jeju Folk Village, and the island's various museums and aquariums — so a surprise monsoon shower doesn't derail your schedule. Alternating between outdoor and indoor stops is the key to a smooth summer Jeju route.

## Insider Tips & Budget Breakdown

Peak summer is when flights, rental cars, and lodging all sell out fast and climb in price. **Late July through early August** is especially fierce, since the whole country is on vacation at once. Book flights at least a month out, and reserve popular stays and rental cars even earlier to be safe. Slipping in a weekday departure can also ease the price sting compared to weekends.

Costs vary by region, but **admission fees** to major sights range from free to around 10,000 won (roughly $7–8). Seongsan Ilchulbong, Bijarim, and Osulloc charge entry, while coastal trails and café streets are free. **Parking** is mostly free or just a small fee, so it's rarely a budget concern. Pad your estimate for meals and cafés, and budget separately for activities like the Udo ferry or a Hallasan hike — that makes planning much simpler.

A few summer essentials round things out. The sun is strong, so **sunscreen, a hat, and sunglasses** are non-negotiable, and during monsoon season an **umbrella or rain poncho** is a must. If you'll need to hail a ride, install a ride-hailing app like Kakao T in advance to make grabbing a taxi far easier. Small preparations like these are what complete a great trip and unlock the best **things to do in Jeju**.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Can I hit all 10 in a 3-day trip?**
Honestly, cramming in all ten is a stretch. For three days, I'd narrow it down and focus on about **six or seven** spots. Pick the highlights — Seongsan Ilchulbong, Seopjikoji, and Udo in the east; a waterfall or Hallasan in the south; Aewol and Osulloc in the west — and travel them at a relaxed pace. You'll enjoy it far more than racing through the whole list. Choosing well beats checking every box.

**Q: Is Jeju doable without a rental car?**
It's possible, but it depends on the region. Jeju City and parts of the east are easily covered by bus and taxi, but **the western outskirts and quieter, off-the-beaten-path sights** have long gaps between buses, so a rental is recommended. If there are two or more of you and your plan leans heavily on outlying spots, a rental car wins on both time and money.

**Q: When's the best (and worst) time for a summer Jeju trip?**
Late June through mid-July is **monsoon season**, so rain is a big wildcard. The upside: fewer crowds and relatively lower prices. Early August is full-blown peak season — busy and pricier. If you want peace and quiet, aim for the tail end of the rainy season; if you want buzz and energy, go for peak summer.

**Q: Which spots are great with kids?**
**Udo** is easy with children since you can cruise it slowly by electric car, and indoor theme parks, aquariums, and museums are perfect regardless of the weather. Strenuous routes like Hallasan are tough with little ones, so build your itinerary around flat, easy walking trails.
