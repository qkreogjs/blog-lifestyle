# Best Places to Visit in Jeju: 10 Must-See Spots You'll Regret Missing

Planning your summer getaway? Now's the time to lock it in. As of late June 2026, Jeju Island has officially entered its monsoon season. Yes, that means scattered rain on plenty of days—but it also means Hallasan's valleys and waterfalls are running full and refreshing, and the moment the rains lift in mid-July, peak beach season kicks off almost overnight. In other words, right now is the smart window to grab your flights and lock down your accommodations before everyone else does.

Whether it's your first trip to South Korea's famous volcanic island or you've been a handful of times but keep looping the same tired route, the dilemma is identical: "With only a few days, where do I start so I don't end up with regrets?" This guide is the answer. We've rounded up the very **best places to visit in Jeju**—organized by region and suitable for families, couples, and friend groups alike—and mapped out a route that fits a perfect three-day, two-night itinerary.

In a destination where a single search returns hundreds of "must-see" spots, the real value is in the curation. Stick to the ten picks and the route below, and you'll have a rich, satisfying trip without wasting time, energy, or money. Read to the end and build your own custom course.

## Why These 10 Spots? (How We Chose)

Out of countless attractions, three criteria guided our list. **First, accessibility.** We prioritized spots you can reach comfortably by rental car or public transit, and that cluster naturally with other sights for an efficient route. **Second, photo payoff.** We weighed whether a place consistently performs on social media—whether a single shot captures the whole mood of your trip. **Third, repeat satisfaction.** We favored spots where travelers keep saying, "I went once and immediately wanted to come back."

We also spread the picks across **the east, west, south, and Jeju City** to cut down on wasted driving. Jeju is deceptively large, and crossing from one side to the other eats up real time—mix regions carelessly and you can lose half a day to the road. So we've grouped nearby spots together.

Finally, we balanced **paid and free** sites and **indoor and outdoor** options. When monsoon showers or a sudden summer downpour scrambles your outdoor plans, you'll want rain-proof indoor backups ready to go. On a Jeju summer trip, where the weather is a genuine wildcard, this matters more than anywhere.

## #1–3: If It's Your First Time in Jeju, These Are Non-Negotiable

**#1 Seongsan Ilchulbong (Sunrise Peak)** is a UNESCO World Natural Heritage site and the icon of eastern Jeju. The crater ridge rising straight out of the sea is genuinely jaw-dropping, and the roughly 20–30 minute climb to the top rewards you with a wide-open panorama. As the name suggests, it's a legendary sunrise spot—but it's plenty stunning by day, too. If you're chasing the sunrise, note that summer days break early, so arrive predawn. The nearby Gwangchigi Beach makes an easy add-on.

**#2 Seopjikoji** sits just about ten minutes by car from Sunrise Peak, making the two an ideal pair. A coastal walking trail, a lighthouse, and architecture made famous by Korean dramas and films come together for first-class photo ops. It gets windy out on the cape, so a hat or a light jacket will keep you comfortable.

**#3 Udo Island** is a tiny islet just a 15-minute ferry ride from Seongsan Port. The standard move is to rent an electric car or scooter and loop the island, and the local specialty—peanut ice cream and peanut makgeolli made from Udo's famous peanuts—is a must-try. Ferries get crowded during the summer peak, so check the round-trip schedule in advance. Clustered in the east, these three are tailor-made for a single-day loop and rank among the very **best places to visit in Jeju**.

## #4–6: The Ultimate Natural-Scenery Course

**#4 Hallasan (Mount Halla)** is the crown jewel of any Jeju trip. The Seongpanak and Gwaneumsa routes to the Baengnokdam summit crater are physically demanding, so beginners should take the **Eorimok** or **Yeongsil** trails instead. Both climb relatively gently to Witse Oreum, making them doable even for families. Eorimok runs a bit longer, while Yeongsil is known for its striking cliff-and-crag scenery. Summer weather can flip fast up here, so always check the entry cutoff times and the forecast before you set out.

**#5 Cheonjiyeon and Jeongbang Waterfalls** handle the "cool and refreshing" department down in southern Seogwipo. Cheonjiyeon is an easy, flat stroll from the entrance, and Jeongbang is one of the rare waterfalls in Asia that plunges directly into the ocean. Monsoon season means heavy water volume, so the falls feel even more powerful—summer is actually a prime time to see them.

**#6 Jungmun Daepo Columnar Joint** showcases hexagonal stone pillars with waves crashing against them—an absolute spectacle. The shots get even more dramatic when the surf is high, so aim for a windy day. Just be sure to shoot from inside the safety railings. If you're after raw, untouched scenery, this course delivers.

## #7–10: Hidden Gems & Aesthetic Spots

**#7 Camellia Hill and Bijarim Forest** are the gold standard for a healing stroll. Camellia Hill is an arboretum bursting with camellias and a huge variety of plants, with a completely different mood each season, while Bijarim is a forest path of centuries-old nutmeg-yew trees—just walking it clears your head. Both are shady and stay relatively cool even in peak summer, making them excellent outdoor escapes from the heat.

**#8 Aewol and Handam Coastal Walk** is a runaway favorite among couples and friend groups. The Handam coastal trail hugs an emerald sea, and the surrounding café district overflows with picture-perfect spots. Shoot it at golden hour with the sunset and it's almost impossibly pretty. As the face of the west coast, it's an essential aesthetic entry among the **best places to visit in Jeju**.

**#9 Osulloc Tea Museum and the Green Tea Fields** lets you take in sweeping tea-plantation views alongside green-tea desserts. Pair it with the adjacent Innisfree Jeju House for an easy half-day course. For **#10**, we'll leave it open as a region-specific local pick. Working a market full of regional eats into your itinerary—like the night market at Dongmun Market or Seogwipo Maeil Olle Market—is a guaranteed way to bump up your trip's satisfaction.

## A 3-Day, 2-Night Suggested Route (Optimized by Region)

Mix regions on Jeju and you'll spend your whole trip on the road. That's why we recommend a clockwise loop running **east → south → west**. **Day one:** head from the airport to the east and bundle Sunrise Peak, Seopjikoji, and Udo, then stay overnight near Seongsan. **Day two:** drop down south for Hallasan (Eorimok or Yeongsil) or the waterfall course, and spend the night in Seogwipo. **Day three:** explore the west—Aewol and Osulloc—before circling back to the airport.

For getting around, a **rental car** is by far the most convenient. Many top sights sit on the outskirts and some routes have long gaps between buses, so if your party is two or more people, a rental car usually wins on both time and cost. On the other hand, if you're solo or not keen on driving, you can lean on buses and taxis while sticking to Jeju City and the east.

Build a **Plan B** for rainy days, too. Scout out indoor options ahead of time—the Osulloc Tea Museum, the Jeju Folk Village, and the island's various indoor museums and aquariums—and a sudden monsoon shower won't derail your schedule. Alternating outdoor and indoor stops is the secret to a smart summer Jeju itinerary.

## Jeju Travel Tips & Budget Breakdown

During the summer peak, flights, rental cars, and accommodations all sell out fast and prices climb. **Late July through early August** is especially fierce, since the whole country is on vacation—expect cutthroat competition for reservations. Book flights at least a month out, and lock in popular stays and rental cars even earlier. Slotting in a weekday departure can ease the price burden compared to weekends.

Costs vary by region, but **admission fees** for major attractions range from free to around 10,000 won (roughly $7–8). Sunrise Peak, Bijarim, and Osulloc charge entry, while coastal walks and café districts are free. **Parking** is mostly free or just a small fee, so it's easy on the wallet. Budget generously for meals and cafés, and tally up activity costs—like the Udo ferry or a Hallasan hike—separately to keep your planning clean.

Don't forget your summer Jeju essentials. The sun is strong, so **sunscreen, a hat, and sunglasses** are baseline, and during monsoon season an **umbrella or rain poncho** is a must. When you need a ride, install an app like Kakao T ahead of time to make hailing a taxi far easier. Little bits of prep like these round out a satisfying trip through the **best places to visit in Jeju**.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Can I hit all ten on a 3-day, 2-night trip?**
Honestly, cramming everything in is a stretch. For three days and two nights, we recommend narrowing your focus to about **six or seven spots** by region. Picking the highlights—Sunrise Peak, Seopjikoji, and Udo in the east; a waterfall or Hallasan in the south; Aewol and Osulloc in the west—and taking your time delivers far more satisfaction than a frantic full sweep. Choose-and-focus beats trying to conquer it all.

**Q: Is a Jeju trip doable without a rental car?**
Doable, but it depends on the region. Jeju City and parts of the east are perfectly manageable by bus and taxi, but **the western outskirts and quieter attractions** have sparse service, so a rental car is recommended there. If you've got two or more people and plan to include a lot of outlying sights, a rental car comes out ahead on both time and cost.

**Q: What's the best season for a summer Jeju trip, and what should I avoid?**
Late June through mid-July is **monsoon** season, so rain is a real variable. The upside: fewer crowds and comparatively lower prices. Early August is full-blown peak season—busy and pricey. If you want quiet, aim for the tail end of the monsoon; if you want buzz and energy, go for peak season.

**Q: Which Jeju spots are good with kids?**
**Udo** is easy to tour slowly by electric car, making it kid-friendly, and indoor theme parks, aquariums, and museums are great regardless of the weather. Strenuous courses like Hallasan are tough with little ones, so lean toward flat walking trails when planning a family route.
